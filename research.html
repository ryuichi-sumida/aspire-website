Our Research

Exploring the dynamics of real human interaction among Deaf signers.

Self-initiated Repair (SIR)

We analyze the Self-initiated Repair sequence in social interactions. This involves how signers correct or adjust their own signing during conversation.

Interactional dynamics in sign language.

The repair segment often includes an adjacency pair or side sequence, such as a question-answer sequence where signers ask interlocutors about unshared vocabulary using an integrated linguistic repertoire: signs, gesturing, fingerspelling, and mouthing.

Improvisational Signing

Improvisational signing highlights that communication is co-constructed. We study how Deaf individuals integrate linguistic and non-linguistic resources into a flexible communicative repertoire.

Example of sign language interaction.

This flexibility is especially evident among Deaf individuals in Japan, who constantly adapt to cultural environments where symbols and communication are not always guaranteed.

Online Cross-signing

It has long been known that deaf people from different countries are able to establish communication with each other quickly (Zeshan, 2015). Our project tracks the development of ad hoc emerging communication right from the beginning.

Case Study: UK and Japan
We observed that successful conveying of numerical expressions was higher in Japan to some extent, but participants from both regions demonstrated skillful use of gestures, International Signs, and other resources to bridge gaps.

Dialogic Sign Language Recognition (DSLR)

The Gap: Existing datasets lack dialogic interaction, relying mostly on monologic (single signer) data.

The Goal: To build technologies capable of recognizing and interpreting sign language in social, interactive contexts. We aim to move from Continuous Sign Language Recognition (CSLR) to Dialogic Sign Language Recognition (DSLR).
